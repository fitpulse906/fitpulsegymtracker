Volume Landmarks Explained: MEV, MAV, and MRV for Smarter Hypertrophy

By Fit Pulse Team11 min readProgramming & Hypertrophy

Ask ten lifters how many sets per week they should perform for a given muscle group, and you will get ten different answers — most of them wrong. The truth is, there is no single magic number. The right amount of training volume is a moving target that depends on your training age, recovery capacity, sleep, nutrition, and the specific muscle in question.

This is where the concept of volume landmarks becomes a game-changer. Pioneered by exercise scientist Dr. Mike Israetel, the framework of MEV, MAV, and MRV gives you a structured, evidence-based way to determine exactly how much work each muscle needs to grow — without overshooting into burnout or undershooting into stagnation.

What Are Volume Landmarks?

Volume landmarks are individual benchmarks that describe how your body responds to weekly training volume, measured in hard sets (sets taken close to muscular failure). Instead of guessing, you treat your training like a dial — too low and nothing happens, too high and recovery collapses, but in the sweet spot, muscle grows efficiently.

There are four key landmarks, but three of them matter most for day-to-day programming.

The Four Volume Landmarks

  • MV (Maintenance Volume): The minimum weekly sets required to maintain your current muscle mass.
  • MEV (Minimum Effective Volume): The smallest dose of weekly sets that actually produces measurable growth.
  • MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume): The range where you get the most muscle growth per unit of effort — the productive sweet spot.
  • MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume): The highest weekly volume you can recover from before performance and growth start to decline.
Quick Definition

A "hard set" is a set taken within 0 to 3 repetitions of muscular failure. Junk volume — easy sets stopped far from failure — does not count toward your landmarks.

Understanding MEV: Your Starting Point

MEV is the floor. Below it, your muscles receive enough stimulus to feel sore but not enough to actually grow. For most intermediate lifters, MEV sits somewhere between 8 to 12 hard sets per muscle per week, though smaller muscles like biceps and calves often need slightly less, while large groups like the back may need more.

Starting a training block at MEV is a powerful strategy. It leaves you plenty of room to add volume over the coming weeks as your body adapts — without front-loading fatigue you cannot recover from.

MAV: The Productive Middle Ground

MAV is where the magic happens. This is the volume range where your body responds most efficiently to training — every additional set still produces meaningful growth. For most muscles, MAV falls between 12 and 20 weekly sets, depending on the individual and the muscle group.

The goal of a well-designed mesocycle is to progress from MEV toward MRV by adding 1 to 3 sets per week, spending most of your training time in the MAV zone before deloading.

Approximate MAV Ranges by Muscle Group

  • Chest: 12 to 20 sets per week
  • Back: 14 to 22 sets per week
  • Quads: 12 to 18 sets per week
  • Hamstrings: 10 to 16 sets per week
  • Shoulders (side delts): 12 to 20 sets per week
  • Biceps and triceps: 10 to 16 sets per week
  • Calves: 10 to 16 sets per week

MRV: The Ceiling You Should Respect

MRV is the upper boundary. Cross it, and you will experience joint pain, performance drops, mood swings, sleep disruption, and stalled progress. Most intermediate lifters hit MRV somewhere between 20 and 28 weekly sets per muscle, though this varies dramatically based on recovery factors.

Signs you have crossed your MRV include strength decreases across multiple sessions, persistent muscle soreness that does not resolve, declining motivation, and trouble falling asleep. When this happens, your only good option is to deload — drop volume back to roughly MEV for one week to let your body fully recover.

Common Mistake

Treating volume landmarks as fixed numbers. Your MRV during a high-stress work week is dramatically lower than your MRV during a vacation. Always adjust based on real-world recovery, not theoretical maximums.

Building a Mesocycle Around Volume Landmarks

A standard hypertrophy mesocycle lasts 4 to 6 weeks and follows a predictable structure: start at MEV, progress through MAV, approach MRV, and then deload. Here is how a 5-week chest block might look:

Sample 5-Week Progression for Chest

  1. Week 1 (MEV): 10 hard sets
  2. Week 2: 12 hard sets
  3. Week 3 (Mid-MAV): 14 hard sets
  4. Week 4 (Approaching MRV): 17 hard sets
  5. Week 5 (Deload): 6 hard sets at lighter weight

The deload week is not optional — it is the mechanism that converts accumulated training stress into actual muscle growth. Skipping deloads is one of the fastest ways to plateau.

How to Find Your Personal Landmarks

The published ranges are educated starting points, not absolutes. Your real landmarks reveal themselves through honest, consistent tracking. This is where a workout tracker becomes essential. Log every set, monitor your recovery between sessions, and watch for objective signals:

  • You are below MEV if: Strength stays flat and you feel barely worked after sessions.
  • You are in MAV if: Strength and reps climb week over week, soreness resolves within 48 hours, and motivation stays high.
  • You are at or above MRV if: Strength drops, joints ache, sleep suffers, and recovery feels impossible.

Practical Programming Tips

Applying volume landmarks does not require complex spreadsheets. A few simple rules will keep you on track:

  • Count only hard sets. Warm-ups and easy back-off sets do not count.
  • Distribute volume across at least two sessions per week. Hitting a muscle once with 18 sets is far less effective than two sessions of 9 sets each.
  • Add volume conservatively. 1 to 3 set increases per week is plenty.
  • Deload every 4 to 6 weeks. Plan it before you need it.
  • Adjust for life stress. Reduce volume during periods of poor sleep, high work demands, or caloric deficit.

Final Thoughts

Volume landmarks transform training from guesswork into strategy. Instead of randomly picking a set count and hoping for the best, you have a framework that respects your individual recovery capacity and progressively challenges your body to adapt. Start at MEV, progress through MAV, respect your MRV, and deload before you need to.

The lifters who build the most muscle are not the ones who train the hardest in any given session — they are the ones who train the smartest across months and years. Track your sets. Listen to your body. Trust the framework.
